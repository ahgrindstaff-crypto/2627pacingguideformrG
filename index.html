Strategic Implementation Manual

Transitioning from Technical Execution to Analytical Justification in Middle School Computer Science

1. Executive Mandate: The Shift from "Doing" to "Justifying"

The objective of this curriculum architecture is a rigorous pivot from the mere production of functional code to the analytical justification of technical artifacts. In an evidence-based STEM pedagogy, successful execution—a robot completing a course or a script running without syntax errors—is no longer the terminal goal. It is merely the baseline.

Mastery is evidenced through documentation that proves a student understands the "why" behind the "how." We are moving students away from "Guess-and-Check" habits and toward an engineering mindset where documentation serves as the primary artifact of learning. This shift requires students to bridge the cognitive gap between Perfect Logic Environments and the Messy Physical World through quantitative evidence and systematic troubleshooting.

The Philosophical Shift

Traditional computer science education often rewards the "finished product," regardless of the haphazard iterations used to achieve it. This manual mandates a transition to Evidence-Based Troubleshooting. Every technical output must be paired with analytical documentation that justifies how the student navigated real-world variables, such as surface friction or sensor drift. Technical artifacts without accompanying justification are considered incomplete and do not meet the threshold for programmatic mastery.

Core Methodologies: The 4 Pillars of Computational Thinking

The strategic transition is anchored in the four pillars, mapped here against specific justification requirements:

Pillar Practical Application in Justification Tasks
Decomposition Breaking complex infrastructure challenges (e.g., the Lakewood Bottleneck) into discrete, observable data points.
Pattern Recognition Identifying recurring physical deficits, such as the "Carpet Tax" or sensor spikes in graphical telemetry.
Abstraction Modeling data payloads across a Multimodal Technical Ecology (iPad to Mac to Chromebook) and identifying system lag.
Algorithmic Thinking Creating modular, reusable procedures utilizing the Applied Geometric Framework (Exterior Angle Rule: 360° ÷ number of sides).

2. The PRIMM Framework and The "PRO" Cycle

To scaffold student metacognition, we implement the PRIMM (Predict, Run, Investigate, Modify, Make) framework. This structure is non-negotiable for moving students from passive code-copying to active systemic analysis.

Predictive Hypotheses: The PRO Cycle

Before a single line of code is executed, students must engage in the Predict-Run-Observe (PRO) cycle. Students must write behavioral hypotheses regarding how their logic will translate into physical movement. This forces students to account for "Physical Deficits"—the reality that battery decay, gear slip, and surface momentum often counteract perfect code.

The Documentation Mandate: The 5-Step Debug Diary

The Debug Diary is the standardized instrument of this curriculum. For every project, students must complete a five-step sheet derived from PRIMM:

1. Predict

Document the expected behavior of the code and hardware.

2. Run

Execute the code and capture the actual outcome.

3. Investigate

Identify the discrepancy. Students must explicitly separate Logical/Syntax Failures (calculation errors, broken references) from Physical/Environmental Failures (accelerometer drift, IR communication interference, or battery depletion).

4. Modify

Detail the precise mathematical or structural tweak applied (e.g., adjusting speed to compensate for "Carpet Tax").

5. Make

Finalize the artifact and confirm the resolution.

3. Vertical Progression: 6th Grade – Foundations and Exploratory Play

The 6th-grade "Exploratory Level" focuses on demystifying systems. Students transition from free-drive play to understanding how block architecture controls physical hardware.

Key Projects and Justifications

  • Sphero Driver’s Test (Exploratory): Moving from manual remote-control to basic macro-blocks. Students collect qualitative data, documenting exactly when and where a robot fails to execute a turn accurately.
  • Multimodal Technical Safari: Students visualize the "Technical Ecology" by mapping a file payload's path—capturing an image on an iPad, AirDropping it to a Mac, and logging the transfer on a Chromebook.
  • Lakewood Bottleneck (Level 1): The first stage of our tri-level spiral. Students use stopwatches to log walking times in a single hallway, calculating a basic class average in Google Sheets to recognize human patterns.

Assessment Artifacts

3-Entry Debug Diary: Basic logging of physical collisions or turn errors.
Google Site Portfolio: Setup of the three-year digital record, featuring screenshots of block architecture and the "Data Path Map."

4. Vertical Progression: 7th Grade – Innovation and System Optimization

In 7th grade, the curriculum shifts toward Quantitative Precision and modularity.

The "Carpet Tax" Audit and Precise Measurement

Students move from "duration-based" travel (seconds) to precise measurement in centimeters (cm). They conduct audits to calculate friction modifiers—known as the Carpet Tax—on tile vs. deep carpet. This requires computing programmatic velocity corrections to ensure 100cm of travel in code equals 100cm of physical movement.

Applied Geometry and Procedural Modularity

Students implement the Exterior Angle Rule (360° ÷ number of sides). Using absolute headings, they create modular procedures (functions) where the number of sides is a dynamic variable, allowing one script to generate any regular polygon Standard: MS-ALG-PS-02.

Engineering Analysis: Lakewood Bottleneck (Level 2)

Students simulate hallway velocities using Google Sheets to find the "breaking point" of the school schedule. This stage introduces Data Cleansing: students must identify and purge "sprint outliers" from their datasets to improve simulation accuracy.

Technical Written Analysis Requirement

A mandatory artifact justifying how software commands for internal motor currents are altered by hardware constraints (friction, momentum, and battery charge).

5. 8th Grade Pathway A: Advanced Automation (Coding) Coding Track

This pathway transitions students to text-based syntax (JavaScript, Python, and Swift via Swift Playgrounds) to manage complex, asynchronous system errors.

Advanced Analytical Artifacts

  • The Live Logging Ecosystem: Students build a "Network Data Dashboard" tracking real-time packet drops and sensor streams (accelerometer, light sensors). They write code exceptions to handle network downtime.
  • Lakewood Bottleneck (Level 3): Students write scripts that auto-generate .csv metrics from their simulations, exporting data directly into Google Sheets analytics endpoints for automated processing.

The Justification Layer

Justification moves to a professional tier, requiring a Software Licensing Manifest and Attribution Registry. Students must distinguish between "learning from others' code" and "plagiarism" during refactoring projects Standard: MS-PRO-PD-14.

6. 8th Grade Pathway B: Human-Centered Systems (DMD) Digital Media Design Track

This pathway analyzes the human element through UI/UX and the Universal Accessibility Framework.

Accessibility Engineering

Students must produce a User Accessibility Audit Log proving compliance with:

  • WCAG Compliance: Cross-demographic usability.
  • Color Contrast Ratios: Optimization for low-vision and color-blind users.
  • Font Legibility Indexes: Semantic readability across different display DPIs.

Data-Driven Visual Architecture

Students utilize the Lakewood Bottleneck simulation data (Level 3) to create Information Architectures. They transform raw .csv logs into density heatmaps and infographics that maintain metric integrity while improving human legibility Standard: MS-ALG-IM-09.

The Constructible Product

Students design 3D templates (cereal boxes, soup cans) using the "Constructible Product Framework." This digital-to-physical translation requires a full Attribution Registry for all used digital assets.

7. Programmatic Compliance and Digital Portfolios

The three-year Google Site Portfolio is the permanent record of student growth. It is not a gallery; it is a Technical Evidence Log. Assessment is contingent upon side-by-side evidence of code architecture vs. physical sensor tracks showing "Reality vs. Prediction."

Final Implementation Summary for Educators

Mandatory Justification: Every technical artifact must have a paired analytical document (Debug Diary or Audit Log).
Tri-Level Lakewood Spiral: Students must progress from manual stopwatches (6th) to spreadsheets (7th) to scripted .csv automation (8th).
Quantitative Precision: Shift from duration-based travel to precise centimeter measurement and friction-coefficient calculation.
